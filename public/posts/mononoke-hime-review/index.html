Princess Mononoke

31/Jan 2016
film

★ ★ ★ ★ ★

Hayao Miyazaki proves he is a true master of storytelling in this battle between nature and humans, love and hate, war and peace, life and death. The story is set during medieval Japan, right at the coming of the Iron Age, where humans either live in peace with nature or die trying to tame it.

In the first moments of the movie, we meet Ashitaka, the prince of a small, peaceful village in the East. Suddenly, the village is under attack by a cursed boar-god with skin made of writhing grey worms. Ashitaka successfully defeats this demon, but in the process, his arm was infected by the same curse that afflicted the boar-god. In order to find a way to lift the curse, he sets off on a journey to the West, where he finds himself in the middle of a war between the forest gods, an iron-working colony, and the samurai enclave nearby.

Miyazaki doesn’t make morality come so easily as there is no clear good vs. evil here; most human characters have two sides to them, and the forest gods are drawn not like “Bambi” animals but like fierce creatures who can and will kill if necessary.

The level of detail in the animation is astounding, and the complexity of the film is unrivaled by other animated films and most live-actions as well. The hand-drawn frames certainly bring an atmospheric quality to the film, and while it might not look as slick as some of the computer-assisted films like “Spirited Away”, the film is still beautiful to watch. This is all supplemented by Joe Hisaishi’s exquisite film score.

It’s a fantastical story of epic proportions. I have probably seen this movie at least a dozen times now, and it never fails to impress me time and time again. Bravo Miyazaki, bravo.

Director: Hayao Miyazaki

PG-13, 134 min.
