Deux Jours, Une Nuit

12 Jan 2016 on Film

★ ★ ★ ★

“Deux Jours, Une Nuit” (Two Days, One Night) reminds us of the people that are negatively affected by the growing wealth gap and the deteriorating middle class. The Dardenne brothers offer yet another important view into the world of the struggling, everyday Belgian worker.

The main character, Sandra (Marion Cotillard), is a young Belgian mother who is recovering from clinical depression. However, she returns from her sick leave to find that her boss has given her 16 coworkers a choice: receive 1000 euro bonuses or let Sandra return and keep her job. Sandra’s coworkers have already voted in favor of receiving bonuses, but the boss agrees to give her job back if Sandra can get a majority to change their mind by Monday, leaving her with just one weekend to track down her colleagues and ask them to change their votes.

It’s a troubling and unfair predicament for everyone. While no one really wants Sandra to lose her job, the 1000 euros could really come in handy for some of these people. What would you say or do?

Unfortunately, the premise of the film naturally lends itself to repetition. Even though every coworker’s circumstances are different, we get the idea by the sixth or seventh coworker: they will either change their vote, or simply say no (sometimes not so politely). The formula works, but eventually all good things succumb to the law of diminishing returns.

But it's the simplicity and the honesty of the social drama that makes “Deux Jours, Une Nuit” a good film. It also helps that nearly every shot includes Cotillard, whose performance deservedly warranted an Oscar nomination. Her weary eyes, heavy walk, and defeatist emotions accurately portray the life of depression. We truly get the feeling that she’s just fighting to stay afloat.

Sometimes, it’s not the story of triumphant success that interests us, but the story of just surviving; something that I’m sure we can all relate to.

Directors: Jean-Pierre Dardenne, Luc Dardenne

Starring: Marion Cotillard, Fabrizio Rongione

PG-13, 95 min
