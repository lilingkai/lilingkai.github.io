Ingeborg Holm

13 Jan 2016 on Film

★ ★ ★ ★

“Ingeborg Holm” is a Swedish social drama based on a play by Nils Krok, hailing from the early days of cinema and the alien planet of silent film.

“But why on earth would you want to watch old, silent, black and white movies? They’re so boring!” Well, I didn’t really have a choice in this case since I’m currently taking History of Film I and I’m responsible for knowing the film. And yes, I understand that most twenty-first century movies are more appealing and more exciting to our modern eyes. But even so, I’m glad to have seen this film, and I hope that I can help some of you better understand and appreciate the merits of silent film.

This is the story of Ingeborg Holm (Hilda Borgstöm), a young Swedish woman who is happily married and a mother to three children. Her husband gets a loan to open their new shop, but things take a grim turn when he suddenly falls ill. Even though I suspect almost none of you will actually watch the film, I cannot spoil the rest of the plot in good conscience. The film’s Wikipedia page provides a conclusive summary if you wish to know the details. All that you need to know is that the film is tragic, and just when you think things couldn’t be any worse… well you’re wrong.

The world of silent black and white film might as well be a different planet to modern audiences; it’s like watching animated grey ghosts who seem so distant from us that it can be hard to relate to characters. But with “Ingeborg Holm”, I was surprised at the clarity of the narrative, told only with visual compositions and a little help from a few title cards. It also helps that Hilda Borgstöm gives an engaging performance as the main lead; I was able to see and feel the emotional pain that she was constantly experiencing. The pacing and directing is all good; with Sjöström’s restraint, the film surprisingly doesn’t descend into some weepy melodrama.

Even though the movie is over a hundred years old, the picture quality of the 16mm film held up surprisingly well, although there were a few spots where the nitrate deterioration is more than noticeable.

It’s definitely not a feel-good kind of movie, but I would recommend it for the fans of the silent genre and hardcore movie watchers looking to gain an encyclopedic knowledge of all things film.

Director: Victor Sjöström

Starring: Hilda Borgström, Aron Lindgren

1913 (Sweden), 96 min
